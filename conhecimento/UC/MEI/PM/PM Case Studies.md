# 17/11/2025
### Microsoft Word
- Massive delay in release (1 year -> 5 years). Initially didn't follow a delivery calendar.
- Unspecified requirements led to a constant change in features (Scope Creep).
- Lifecycle was initially **ad-hoc**, then **waterfall**, then **zero-defects**.
- Changes in leadership and personnel, due to massive burnout caused by constantly changing deadlines and requirements.
- Code full of errors (**infinite-defects**) due to the speed at which the developers needed to work (no time to test), mixed with the fact that they relied heavily on superstar developers at the start, which wound up making the code rather hard to understand, impeding future progress.
- Code sharing with MacWord wound up creating massive delays as well, as it wasn't stable and changes in the original code often added more changes in requirements.

### Denver Bag System
- When building the Denver airport, a massive luggage transportation was designed to completely revolutionize this field, though several aspects were completely overlooked.
- Work on designing this system started only two years before the airport was set to open, which was very little time for a project of this scale. This caused the airport to miss its open date by 16 months, opening in March 1995.
- The system itself was poorly designed, many safety and operational concerns were only thought of too late during the process, which caused for an emergency need to drastically reduce the scope of the process. 
- Notably, the system didn't include a backup system for if its incredibly complex functionalities didn't work for a reason or another.
- A massive lesson in requirement definition, estimation, risk assessment, maintaining the Magic Four.

### Tenerife Plane Crash
- Bomb threat in main airport caused several planes to go to smaller airport.
- Several things went wrong: 
  - Bomb threat
  - Airport had little resources for this many airplanes, from being small, to not having a ground radar, to being understaffed
  - Extremely low visibility due to fog, and lights were off that day
  - Everybody was late and the KLM pilot decided to refuel there, which caused even more delay, building need for quick action
  - Pan Am pilot was instructed to turn at 3rd exit but couldn't do so because the turn was too sharp
  - KLM misheard the indications from control tower due to a rare audio phenomenon during radio communication.
  - Confusing, non-standard messages.
  - A final warning was ignored by the KLM captain and the copilot was too scared to say anything.
- Teaches important lessons about communication, risk handling.

# 15/12/2025
### **The Army Crew Team**
- May 2002
- Junior Varsity rowing team consistently beat the Varsity team in practice, even though the latter had the clear better athletes. In smaller groups, the V team beat the JV team, but the full JV group beat the V team 2/3 of the times.
- V boat had superior individual skills, but lacked teamwork, coordination, and synchronization. They were critical of each other, had several "team disrupters", were afraid to lose and embarrassed by JV, displayed self-doubt.
- JV boat had lower skills, but displayed great teamwork, encouragement, and synchronization. They trusted each other, were positive and motivated ("nothing to lose" mentality), were supportive instead of pointing fingers, had no "team disrupters".
- Coach intervenes with a confrontational meeting ahead of the tournament, which pushes the team into crisis.
- **Main Lesson:** Team dynamics, trust and psychological factors often outweigh individual talent. Exceptional-skill individuals don't automatically create an amazing team.
- This case is a powerful reminder that in **project management**, **people and processes** are just as important as **technical skills and tools**. A high-performing team is more than the sum of its parts, it’s about **how those parts work together**.
#### PM Lessons
###### **Team Composition vs. Team Performance**
In PM, you often select team members based on **skills, experience, and technical ability**. However, this case shows that **team dynamics, communication, and psychological safety** can outweigh individual talent. 
**PM Takeaway:** Don’t just build a team of “stars.” Build a team that **works well together**.
###### **Leadership and Coaching**
Coach P. is like a **project manager**—he selects the team, sets the strategy, and tries to optimize performance. His failure to address **team dysfunction** early leads to ongoing poor performance.
**PM Takeaway:** A PM must not only manage tasks but also **manage relationships, morale, and conflict**.
###### **Psychological Factors and Team Culture**
The study mentioned in the case shows that **master coaches focus on psychological variables**. The JV boat had a **supportive, trusting culture** (“nothing to lose”), while the Varsity boat had **blame, criticism, and low trust**.
**PM Takeaway:** Foster a **positive team culture**—encourage collaboration, not blame.
###### **Metrics vs. Reality**
Coach P. relied heavily on **objective metrics** (erg times, seat racing) to form his team. But these metrics didn’t capture **team synergy, communication, or motivation**.
**PM Takeaway:** Use both **quantitative and qualitative measures** to assess team health and performance.
###### **Conflict and Communication**
Varsity team members sent **negative, blaming emails**. JV team members sent **supportive, unified messages**.
**PM Takeaway:** Encourage **open, respectful communication** and address conflicts early.
###### **Change Management and Decision-Making**
Coach P. considers **drastic changes** (swapping boats, switching members) late in the season.
This reflects the **challenge of making tough decisions under pressure** in a project.
**PM Takeaway:** Sometimes you must **reorganize teams or redefine roles** mid-project, but do so carefully and with team input.

### **Project Closing**
- Start -> Initiate -> (Plan -> Execute / Monitor -> Control) -> **CLOSE** -> End
- Closing is the stage where all deliverables are finalized, and all documentation is signed off, approved, and archived.
- You enter this phase when all project objectives are met or the client terminates the project (This links to monitoring **KPIs and milestones** to objectively know when you're done).
- Low activity, just like initiation
- **Why?** Wrap up, Move on, Get paid
- **Steps before closing:**
	- **Review lessons learned:** important throughout project but mostly at the end so we can apply them to future projects
	- **Get approvals / signatures:** Make sure stakeholders approve of what has been done in the project, use signatures to legally bind
	- **Close contracts:** When they are no longer needed due to the end of the project
	- **Compute final costs** and **make payments**
	- **Finalize reports and documentation.** Once it's done, **archive it** for future access. 
	- **Transition:** Take care of handling who is going to manage the finished project after it is ended by giving them what is necessary for such
	- **Release resources:** For work on future projects, or for the public
#### Notes from Project Management Institute (PMI)
**Project Closing** is the formal process to verify all work is complete, all processes have been executed, and to obtain official recognition that the project is finished. It's not automatic; it requires deliberate action. Many project managers view project success solely as delivering on time and on budget, treating the Closing process as an administrative burden of little value. This is a critical mistake. 

**Failure to conduct** a thorough Project Closing can:
1. **Expose the organization to significant risks and financial losses** (e.g., unpaid contracts, regulatory non-compliance).
2. **Prevent the organization from realizing the project's intended benefits.**
3. **Undermine the credibility** of the project manager and team.
4. Lead to two common dysfunctional scenarios:
	- **The Never-Ending Project:** The project team (skilled at _development_) is stuck maintaining deliverables, consuming resources needed for new projects.
	- **The Orphan Product:** Deliverables are not properly handed over to operations, leading to a lack of support, training, and owner accountability.

**A proper close-out includes**:
- Verifying all work is done and obtaining formal sponsor/customer acceptance.
- Making sure organizational governance and project management processes were followed.
- Administratively closing all procurements and contracts.
- Validating that the project's benefits (from the business case) have been achieved.
- **Capturing lessons learned** (what went well, what could be improved) to build organizational knowledge and improve future projects.
- Disbanding the project team and formally transitioning deliverables to operations.

**When to close:** Best practice is to conduct closing **at the end of every major project phase**, not just at the end. These "phase-gate reviews" allow teams to:
- Get formal acceptance for completed phases, preventing scope creep.
- Validate that the business case is still relevant and the project is on track to meet it.
- Reassess assumptions and constraints.
- Capture lessons learned while they're fresh.
- Refine plans for the next phase with better information.

**Steps:**
1. **Ensure a proper handover** to operations (training, tools, documentation).
2. **Verify strategic goals** are met.
3. **Obtain formal, documented acceptance** of the final deliverable.
4. **Close all contracts** (ensure all obligations and payments are complete).
5. **Review project management practices.**
6. **Document final lessons learned.**
7. **Officially disband the project team.**

#### Project Closure Report
**Formal, written assessment** that marks the official end of a project. It has two purposes:
1. **Proof of Delivery:** To document that the team delivered what was promised (scope, timeline, budget).    
2. **Learning Tool:** To analyze what worked and what didn't, providing insights for future projects.

**Must include:**
- **Administrative & Legal Closure:**
    - Original requirements, timeline, and budget.
    - **Proof** each requirement was met (using project data).
    - Confirmation all payments to/from vendors are settled.
    - Release or transfer of project resources.
    - Proper offboarding of contractors/freelancers.
- **Knowledge & Feedback Capture:**
    - **Lessons learned** and client feedback.
    - A holistic **performance review** of major project sections.
- **Archiving & Handoff:**
    - Organized archive of all project files and communications.
    - Confirmation the client received all deliverables.

**Importance of Closing Strategies:**
- **Analysis:** They help analyze the _"why"_ behind project outcomes (successes and failures).
- **Knowledge Transfer:** The report is shared with stakeholders, clients, and team members, providing a shared reference point.
- **Justification for Resources:** Data from the report can justify requests for better tools, more team members, or improved resource allocation.
- **Continuous Improvement:** It's the primary tool for learning and improving processes for similar future projects. Does this by:
	- **Comparing projected vs. actual** performance (time, cost, goals) and investigate significant variances.
	- **Identifying recurring roadblocks** and create plans to avoid them.
	- **Analyzing resource allocation** to improve workload distribution.
	- **Reviewing communication efficiency** and improve systems (e.g., using integrated project management software).

**Main takeaways:**
- **Formal Sign-Off:** Project closure is not informal; it requires a **formal report** and **documented acceptance**.
- **Beyond Delivery:** Success isn't just delivering the output; it's also about **capturing lessons** and **tying up all administrative/contractual loose ends**.
- **Basis for Improvement:** The closure report is the **primary input for organizational process assets** and updating best practices.
- **Checklist is Crucial:** Memorizing the core components of the closure checklist is essential for both exams and real-world practice.

#### Detailed Timeline
**Phase: Project Handoff & Formal Acceptance**
- **Step 1: Finalize & Transfer Deliverables**
    - **Action:** Review the project plan and scope document.
    - **Action:** Ensure every listed deliverable is 100% complete.
    - **Action:** Officially hand over all deliverables to the client/end-user.
    - **Output:** All deliverables are physically or digitally transferred.
- **Step 2: Obtain Formal Project Sign-Off**
    - **Action:** Present deliverables for final stakeholder review.
    - **Action:** Obtain documented approval/sign-off from the client/sponsor confirming all work is accepted as per the plan.
    - **Critical:** This step formally ends the project work and prevents future "scope creep" or change requests.
    - **Output:** Signed project acceptance document.
**Phase: Administrative & Contractual Closure**
- **Step 3: Review & Close All Contracts and Finances**
    - **Action:** Audit all contracts with suppliers, contractors, and vendors.
    - **Action:** Confirm all services/products were received and all invoices are paid.
    - **Action:** Resolve any outstanding contractual obligations (e.g., release performance bonds, collect warranties).
    - **Output:** Contract closure confirmation, financial reconciliation report.
- **Step 4: Release Project Resources**
    - **Action:** Notify all team members, contractors, and partners of the project's end.
    - **Action:** Officially release them from the project, freeing them for other work.
    - **Action:** Complete any final HR or logistical paperwork.
    - **Output:** Resource release notifications, team disbanded.
**Phase: Review, Learn, & Archive**
- **Step 5: Conduct Project Post-Mortem / Lessons Learned Review**
    - **Action:** Gather the core project team for a review meeting.
    - **Action:** **Analyze Performance:** Compare final results to original **Cost, Schedule, and Quality/Scope** baselines.
    - **Action:** **Gather Team Feedback:** Discuss what went well, challenges, communication effectiveness, and client satisfaction.
    - **Action: Identify Lessons Learned:** Document specific successes to repeat and failures to avoid.
    - **Output:** Post-mortem report with performance data and documented lessons learned.
- **Step 6: Archive All Project Documentation**
    - **Action:** Compile and organize all project documents (charter, plan, logs, reports, contracts, communications).
    - **Action:** Index and store them in a company archive or knowledge management system for future reference.
    - **Why:** Creates a legal paper trail and a resource for future projects.
    - **Output:** A complete, organized project archive.
**Phase: Formal Closure & Recognition**
- **Step 7: Celebrate & Recognize the Team**
    - **Action:** Hold a final team meeting or event to acknowledge the hard work and formally close the project socially.
    - **Impact:** Boosts morale, rewards effort, and encourages future collaboration.
    - **Output:** Team celebration, project officially concluded.
