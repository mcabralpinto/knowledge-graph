*Simplified representation of a [[System|system]].*

- Can have different **types**: *conceptual*, *verbal*, *diagrammatical*, *physical*, *formal*.
- There are **Mathematical**, **Agent-Based**, and **Network** models (etc.).
- Good models are **simple** (Occam's Razor), **explanatory**, **predictive**, **robust**, and **evolvable**.
## Process
1. Observe the system, **Collect data** 
2. Determine the possible rules that **explain the system**’s characteristics 
3. Use the rules to **make predictions** and compare them with the reality 
4. **Modify and repeat** if not satisfied
## Models & Complex Systems
Models define a mathematical model of the structure (components, interconnections) and the rules of change. They allow for studying their behavior. For example, in:
- **Mathematical Models:** difference/differential equations, cellular automata, etc.
- **Agent-Based Models:** define agents, their behaviors, and interactions.
- **Network Models:** define nodes, edges, and their properties.
## Classification
- **Discrete vs. Continuous:** Discrete models represent systems with distinct states or time steps, while continuous models represent systems with continuous variables and time.
- **State Variable Amount:** Models can be classified based on the number of state variables they include, such as single-variable models, multi-variable models, or high-dimensional models (e.g., the interest in a bank account $x_t = (1+k)x_{t.-1}$ vs. a ball throw's equations $h(t) =~...$ and $v(t) =~...$).
- **Linear vs. Non-linear:** Linear models assume a linear relationship between variables, while non-linear models allow for more complex relationships.
- **Autonomous vs. Non-autonomous:** Autonomous models do not explicitly depend on time, while non-autonomous models include time as a variable.
